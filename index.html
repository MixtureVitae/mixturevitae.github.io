Why this matters

The strongest open pretraining corpora—FineWeb-Edu, DCLM—rely on mixed-license or unspecified web data. Strictly permissive alternatives exist but tend to lag on reasoning-heavy tasks. This forces practitioners into an uncomfortable choice: performance or auditability.

MixtureVitae is built for researchers who want stronger legal provenance without giving up competitive downstream performance. It combines explicitly permissive and public-domain sources with narrowly justified risk-mitigated additions, then front-loads reasoning and instruction density to improve capability in small models.

The key design insight: reasoning and instruction signals are typically scarce in strictly permissive web corpora and are usually reserved for post-training. MixtureVitae builds them into the pretraining mixture itself—all generated from permissive seeds using permissively licensed models—and shows this is the primary driver of capability gains.

One-sentence summary. MixtureVitae is a 422B-token, permissive-first, risk-mitigated pretraining dataset that uses dense instruction and reasoning data plus provenance-aware curation to achieve strong small-model results, with decontamination and ablation evidence supporting the claims.

Dataset

MixtureVitae is built from three primary categories of sources: diverse web data for language and general knowledge, curated domain-specific corpora, and—most distinctively—a large block of instruction and reasoning datasets that would normally appear only at post-training.

Figure 1. Corpus composition by top-level category (left) and governing license (right). The dataset totals 422B tokens across web (34.5B), curated (210.6B), and reasoning & instructions (177.2B). The largest license slice is CC-BY-4.0 at 147.9B tokens (35.0%), followed by Apache at 122.7B (29.0%).

Three provenance tiers

Every source in MixtureVitae is assigned to one of three tiers reflecting its legal risk profile. This tiering is risk-mitigated, not risk-free—copyright regimes vary across jurisdictions, and Tier 2/Tier 3 users should consult local counsel where permissibility is jurisdiction-specific.

Results

All models are trained using the open-sci-ref protocol—fixed architecture, fixed hyperparameters, only the dataset varies—so performance differences are attributable solely to the data. Evaluations cover 130M–1.7B parameters at 50B and 300B token budgets.

General benchmarks — competitive with mixed-license baselines, clear leader among permissive ones

At 1.7B parameters / 300B tokens: MixtureVitae avg 0.56, matching DCLM (0.56) and surpassing FineWeb-Edu (0.55), Comma-0.1 (0.50), and CommonCorpus (0.47). On MMLU, MixtureVitae scores 0.38—most permissive and non-permissive baselines remain near random chance (0.25–0.27). On BoolQ: 0.75.
Figure 3. Average performance across 11 tasks (left) and MMLU (right) for a 1.7B model as training progresses to 300B tokens. MixtureVitae (green) leads all permissive baselines throughout and approaches or matches DCLM and FineWeb-Edu by 300B tokens.

Math, code, and instruction — a different class of results

GSM8K: 0.53 · HumanEval: 0.32 · MBPP: 0.38
Every other dataset evaluated at 300B tokens scores 0.02–0.06 on GSM8K and caps at 0.13 on HumanEval. The 1.7B MixtureVitae base model exceeds SmolLM2-1.7B-Instruct—trained on ≈11T tokens (36× more)—on all three benchmarks.
Table 2. Performance on GSM8K, HumanEval, MBPP, and IF-Eval for 1.7B models. MixtureVitae (300B tokens) is compared to all open-sci-ref baselines at 300B tokens, baselines extended to 1T tokens, and post-trained reference models SmolLM2-1.7B and SmolLM2-1.7B-Instruct.

These gains are not from post-training—the compared model is a base model. The large block of permissive-first, synthetic reasoning and instruction data baked into the pretraining mixture provides capabilities that typically require a separate supervised fine-tuning stage.

More evaluations (not decontaminated — treat as exploratory)

Additional benchmarks (e.g. harder reasoning tasks) appear in Appendix E of the paper. The paper notes those additional evaluations were not explicitly decontaminated and should be interpreted with appropriate caution. The headline results above—GSM8K, HumanEval, MBPP, MMLU, BoolQ, ARC—are all fully decontaminated.

Integrity

Strong benchmark numbers on a dataset with heavy instruction content invite two natural questions: Is this contamination? And what specifically drives the gains? Both are addressed directly.

Decontamination

A full 13-gram exact-match decontamination sweep was run between MixtureVitae and all evaluated benchmarks. Overlapping items were removed to create decontaminated test sets, and models were retrained after removing the shards responsible for the majority of detected overlap.

GSM8K: 0.54 decontaminated vs. 0.53 original. MBPP: 0.38 vs. 0.38. Scores are stable. The training trajectory of the retrained model tracks closely with the full model.

Figure 4. Training trajectory of a 1.7B model at 100B tokens: full MixtureVitae (green) versus the version retrained after removing contamination-heavy shards (purple dashed). The curves are nearly identical, confirming results are not an artifact of test-set leakage.

Ablation

To isolate which components drive the gains, three 100B-token models were trained: full MixtureVitae, without the web component, and without the instruction/reasoning component.

Removing instruction/reasoning data causes the largest drop by far. GSM8K falls from 0.47 → 0.03 without instructions; removing web data only drops it to 0.41. The web component contributes, but the instruction and reasoning data is the primary causal driver of math and code gains.

Figure 5. Left: average 10-task performance during training for the three ablation configurations. Right: GSM8K, MBPP, and IF-Eval breakdowns. Removing instructions (orange) causes a dramatic collapse on math and code while removing web (blue) has a much smaller effect.
An additional ablation removing only Tier 2(b) components—synthetic data from non-permissive generators or opaque seeds—produces a training trajectory indistinguishable from the full model. Users with strict compliance requirements can safely exclude Tier 2(b) without measurable quality loss.

FAQ

What does "permissive-first" mean?

Permissive-first means the primary inclusion criterion for any source is its legal and licensing status, not its quality score or popularity. A source is considered only if it carries a clear permissive license (CC-BY, Apache 2.0, MIT, public domain) or falls into a narrowly justified risk-mitigated category (government works, EU TDM-eligible sources). Quality and capability are secondary filters applied within the permissive set.

This is in contrast to most web-scale corpora, which start from broad crawls and retroactively filter out bad content—without filtering for licensing status.

What are the three provenance tiers?

Tier 1 (75.0%, 315.8B tokens): explicit open licenses and public domain. Text under CC0, CC-BY, Apache 2.0, MIT, BSD, or statutory public domain. Synthetic data from permissively licensed models and seeds. Minimal legal risk.

Tier 2 (21.0%, 88.6B tokens): curated permissive repositories with some upstream opacity. Tier 2a includes resources like The Stack v1 and Wikipedia-derived corpora where provenance is partially tracked but not fully auditable at the example level. Tier 2b (~4% of total) is synthetic data where the generator or seed has restricted or opaque provenance—this subset is isolated and can be excluded without performance loss.

Tier 3 (4.0%, 16.8B tokens): civic and governmental works. Materials that are statutory public domain (US federal works) or created for broad public dissemination (government websites, regulatory notices). Further filtered with copyright keyword checks.

Is the dataset fully risk-free?

No. The paper explicitly uses the term risk-mitigated, not risk-free. The tiering rests primarily on US fair-use doctrine and the EU Text and Data Mining exception. Copyright regimes differ across jurisdictions—international adopters should consult local counsel before deploying Tier 2 or Tier 3 subsets in settings where their permissibility is jurisdiction-specific.

Users with strict compliance requirements can restrict to Tier 1 sources only. The shard-level provenance metadata (released at acceptance) is designed to enable exactly this kind of risk-aware subsetting.

Why is there so much instruction and reasoning data?

Strictly permissive web corpora are nearly devoid of the kind of structured reasoning and instruction-following examples that appear in human-written QA, math solutions, and code explanations. This data is typically introduced during post-training (SFT, RLHF), but post-training can't fully recover what pretraining never saw.

MixtureVitae front-loads this signal—177.2B tokens of reasoning and instruction data, all generated from permissive seeds using permissively licensed models—into the pretraining mix. The ablation confirms this is the primary driver of the GSM8K, HumanEval, and MBPP gains: removing it drops GSM8K from 0.47 to 0.03.

How was contamination checked?

A 13-gram exact-match decontamination sweep was run between MixtureVitae and all evaluated benchmarks. Document-level overlap is at or below 0.0003% for ARC, HellaSwag, LAMBADA, OpenBookQA, and PIQA. Code benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP) have higher but still small rates.

Two additional tests were run: (1) re-evaluation on test sets with all overlapping items removed— scores remain stable (GSM8K 0.54 vs 0.53 original); (2) retraining a 1.7B model after removing shards responsible for the majority of contamination—the training trajectory is nearly identical to the full model.

How does MixtureVitae compare to Comma-0.1 and CommonCorpus?

Both Comma-0.1 and CommonCorpus are also permissive baselines. Key differences:

Token uniqueness: Comma-0.1's ≈1T training tokens contain only ≈316B unique tokens due to up to 6× repetition. MixtureVitae contains 422B unique tokens.

Design: The Common Pile (the source corpus for Comma-0.1) deliberately avoids synthetic data. MixtureVitae augments permissive sources with permissive-first synthetic reasoning and instruction data organized into explicit legal-risk tiers.

Outcome: At 1.7B / 300B tokens, MixtureVitae averages 0.56 vs. Comma-0.1's 0.50 on general benchmarks, and dramatically outperforms on reasoning and code—GSM8K 0.53 vs. 0.06, HumanEval 0.32 vs. 0.13, MBPP 0.38 vs. 0.22.
